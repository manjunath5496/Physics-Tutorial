<h2><span id="What_type_of_subject_is_physics?" class="mw-headline">What&nbsp;type&nbsp;of subject is physics?</span></h2>
<p>Physics can be described as all of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>An&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Academic discipline" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_discipline">academic discipline</a>&nbsp;&ndash; one with academic departments, curricula and degrees; national and international societies; and specialized journals.</li>
<li>A scientific field (a branch of&nbsp;<a title="Science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science">science</a>) &ndash; widely recognized category of specialized expertise within science, and typically embodies its own terminology and nomenclature. Such a field will usually be represented by one or more scientific journals, where peer-reviewed research is published.
<ul>
<li>A&nbsp;<a title="Natural science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science">natural science</a>&nbsp;&ndash; one that seeks to elucidate the rules that govern the&nbsp;<a title="Nature" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature">natural</a>&nbsp;world using&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical method" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_method">empirical</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Scientific method" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method">scientific method</a>.
<ul>
<li>A&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Physical science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_science">physical science</a>&nbsp;&ndash; one that studies non-living systems.</li>
<li>A&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Biological science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_science">biological science</a>&nbsp;&ndash; one that studies the role of physical processes in living organisms<em>.</em></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Branches_of_physics" class="mw-headline">Branches of physics</span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a title="Outline of astronomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_astronomy">Astronomy</a>&nbsp;&ndash; studies the universe beyond Earth, including its formation and development, and the evolution, physics, chemistry, meteorology, and motion of celestial objects (such as galaxies, planets, etc.) and phenomena that originate outside the atmosphere of Earth (such as the cosmic background radiation).
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Astrodynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrodynamics">Astrodynamics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; application of ballistics and celestial mechanics to the practical problems concerning the motion of rockets and other spacecraft.</li>
<li><a title="Astrometry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometry">Astrometry</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of astronomy that involves precise measurements of the positions and movements of stars and other celestial bodies.</li>
<li><a title="Astrophysics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics">Astrophysics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the physical aspects of celestial objects</li>
<li><a title="Celestial mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics">Celestial mechanics</a>&nbsp;- the branch of theoretical astronomy that deals with the calculation of the motions of celestial objects such as planets.</li>
<li><a title="Extragalactic astronomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_astronomy">Extragalactic astronomy</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of astronomy concerned with objects outside our own Milky Way Galaxy</li>
<li><a title="Galactic astronomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_astronomy">Galactic astronomy</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of our own Milky Way galaxy and all its contents.</li>
<li><a title="Physical cosmology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology">Physical cosmology</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the largest-scale structures and dynamics of the universe and is concerned with fundamental questions about its formation and evolution.</li>
<li><a title="Planetary science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_science">Planetary science</a>&nbsp;&ndash; scientific study of planets (including Earth), moons, and planetary systems, in particular those of the Solar System and the processes that form them.</li>
<li><a title="Astronomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#Stellar_astronomy">Stellar astronomy</a>&nbsp;&ndash; natural science that deals with the study of celestial objects (such as stars, planets, comets, nebulae, star clusters and galaxies) and phenomena that originate outside the atmosphere of Earth (such as cosmic background radiation)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a title="Atmospheric physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_physics">Atmospheric physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the application of physics to the atmosphere</li>
<li><a title="Atomic, molecular, and optical physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic,_molecular,_and_optical_physics">Atomic, molecular, and optical physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of how matter and light interact
<ul>
<li><a title="Optics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics">Optics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physics which involves the behavior and properties of light, including its interactions with matter and the construction of instruments that use or detect it.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a title="Biophysics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biophysics">Biophysics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; interdisciplinary science that uses the methods of physics to study biological systems
<ul>
<li><a title="Neurophysics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurophysics">Neurophysics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of biophysics dealing with the nervous system.</li>
<li><a title="Polymer physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer_physics">Polymer physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; field of physics that studies polymers, their fluctuations, mechanical properties, as well as the kinetics of reactions involving degradation and polymerisation of polymers and monomers respectively.</li>
<li><a title="Quantum biology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_biology">Quantum biology</a>&nbsp;- application of&nbsp;<a title="Quantum mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>&nbsp;to biological phenomenon.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a title="Chemical physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_physics">Chemical physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physics that studies chemical processes from the point of view of physics.</li>
<li><a title="Computational physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_physics">Computational physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study and implementation of numerical algorithms to solve problems in physics for which a quantitative theory already exists.</li>
<li><a title="Condensed matter physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics">Condensed matter physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the physical properties of condensed phases of matter.</li>
<li><a title="Electricity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity">Electricity</a>&nbsp;&ndash; the study of electrical phenomena.</li>
<li><a title="Electromagnetism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism">Electromagnetism</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of science concerned with the forces that occur between electrically charged particles.</li>
<li><a title="Geophysics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geophysics">Geophysics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; the physics of the Earth and its environment in space; also the study of the Earth using quantitative physical methods</li>
<li><a title="Magnetism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism">Magnetism</a>&nbsp;&ndash; the study of physical phenomena that are mediated by&nbsp;<a title="Magnetic field" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field">magnetic field</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Mathematical physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics">Mathematical physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; application of mathematics to problems in physics and the development of mathematical methods for such applications and for the formulation of physical theories.</li>
<li><a title="Mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics">Mechanics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physics concerned with the behavior of physical bodies when subjected to forces or displacements, and the subsequent effects of the bodies on their environment.
<ul>
<li><a title="Aerodynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the motion of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Air" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air">air</a>.</li>
<li><a title="Biomechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomechanics">Biomechanics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the structure and function of biological systems such as humans, animals, plants, organs, and cells by means of the methods of mechanics.</li>
<li><a title="Classical mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics">Classical mechanics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; one of the two major sub-fields of mechanics, which is concerned with the set of physical laws describing the motion of bodies under the action of a system of forces.
<ul>
<li><a title="Kinematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics">Kinematics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of&nbsp;<a title="Classical mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics">classical mechanics</a>&nbsp;that describes the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Motion (physics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(physics)">motion</a>&nbsp;of points, bodies (objects) and systems of bodies (groups of objects) without consideration of the causes of motion.
<ul>
<li><a title="Homeokinetics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeokinetics">Homeokinetics</a>&nbsp;- the physics of complex, self-organizing systems</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a title="Continuum mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics">Continuum mechanics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of mechanics that deals with the analysis of the kinematics and the mechanical behavior of materials modeled as a continuous mass rather than as discrete particles.</li>
<li><a title="Dynamics (mechanics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)">Dynamics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the causes of motion and changes in motion</li>
<li><a title="Fluid mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_mechanics">Fluid mechanics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of fluids and the forces on them.
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Fluid statics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_statics">Fluid statics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of fluids at rest</li>
<li><a title="Fluid kinematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_kinematics">Fluid kinematics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of fluids in motion</li>
<li><a title="Fluid dynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics">Fluid dynamics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; study of the effect of forces on fluid motion</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a title="Statics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statics">Statics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of mechanics concerned with the analysis of loads (force, torque/moment) on physical systems in static equilibrium, that is, in a state where the relative positions of subsystems do not vary over time, or where components and structures are at a constant velocity.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a title="Statistical mechanics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics">Statistical mechanics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physics which studies any physical system that has a large number of degrees of freedom.</li>
<li><a title="Thermodynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physical science concerned with heat and its relation to other forms of energy and work.</li>
<li><a title="Nuclear physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics">Nuclear physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; field of physics that studies the building blocks and interactions of atomic nuclei.</li>
<li><a title="Particle physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics">Particle physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physics that studies the properties and interactions of the fundamental constituents of matter and energy.</li>
<li><a title="Psychophysics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychophysics">Psychophysics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; quantitatively investigates the relationship between physical stimuli and the sensations and perceptions they affect.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Plasma physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_physics">Plasma physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; the study of plasma, a state of matter similar to gas in which a certain portion of the particles are ionized.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics">Quantum physics</a>&nbsp;&ndash; branch of physics dealing with physical phenomena where the action is on the order of the Planck constant.</li>
<li><a title="Theory of relativity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity">Relativity</a>&nbsp;&ndash; theory of physics which describes the relationship between space and time.
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="General Relativity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Relativity">General Relativity</a>&nbsp;- the geometric theory of gravitation the current description of gravitation in modern physics.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" title="Special Relativity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_Relativity">Special Relativity</a>&nbsp;- a theory that describes the propagation of matter and light at high speeds.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<h2><span id="General_concepts_of_physics" class="mw-headline">General concepts of physics</span></h2>
<h3><span id="Basic_principles_of_physics" class="mw-headline">Basic principles of physics</span></h3>
<p>Physics &ndash; branch of science that studies&nbsp;<a title="Matter" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter">matter</a>&nbsp;and its&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Motion (physics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(physics)">motion</a>&nbsp;through&nbsp;<a title="Spacetime" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime">space and time</a>, along with related concepts such as&nbsp;<a title="Energy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy">energy</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Force" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force">force</a>.&nbsp;Physics is one of the "<a class="mw-redirect" title="Fundamental science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_science">fundamental sciences</a>" because the other natural sciences (like biology, geology etc.) deal with systems that seem to obey the laws of physics. According to physics, the physical laws of matter, energy and the fundamental forces of nature govern the interactions between particles and physical entities (such as planets, molecules, atoms or the subatomic particles). Some of the basic pursuits of physics, which include some of the most prominent developments in modern science in the last millennium, include:</p>

<li>Describing the nature, measuring and quantifying of bodies and their motion, dynamics etc.

<h2><span id="A" class="mw-headline">A</span></h2>
<dl class="glossary">
<dt id="ab_initio" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Ab initio methods (nuclear physics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ab_initio_methods_(nuclear_physics)">ab initio</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A mathematical model which seeks to describe&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#nucleus"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;nucleus">atomic nuclei</span></a>&nbsp;by solving the non-relativistic&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#schr%C3%B6dinger_equation"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;Schr&ouml;dinger equation">Schr&ouml;dinger equation</span></a>&nbsp;for all constituent&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#nucleon"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;nucleon">nucleons</span></a>&nbsp;and the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#nuclear_force"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;nuclear force">forces</span></a>&nbsp;that exist between them. Such methods yield precise results for very light nuclei but become more approximate for heavier nuclei.</dd>
<dt id="abbe_number" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Abbe number" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbe_number">Abbe number</a></dfn></dt>
</dl>
<p class="glossary-hatnote"><span class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">Also called the&nbsp;<strong>V-number</strong>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<strong>constringence</strong>.</span></p>
<dl class="glossary">
<dd class="glossary">In&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#optics"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;optics">optics</span></a>&nbsp;and lens design, a measure of a transparent material's&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#dispersion"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;dispersion">dispersion</span></a>&nbsp;(a variation of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#refractive_index"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;refractive index">refractive index</span></a>&nbsp;versus&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#wavelength"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;wavelength">wavelength</span></a>). High values of V indicate low dispersion.</dd>
<dt id="absolute_electrode_potential" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Absolute electrode potential" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_electrode_potential">absolute electrode potential</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">In&nbsp;<a title="Electrochemistry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, the electrode potential of a metal measured with respect to a universal reference system (without any additional metal&ndash;solution interface).</dd>
<dt id="absolute_humidity" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Absolute humidity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_humidity">absolute humidity</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The ratio of the water vapor in a sample of air to the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#volume"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;volume">volume</span></a>&nbsp;of the sample.</dd>
<dt id="absolute_magnitude" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Absolute magnitude" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude">absolute magnitude</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Is a measure of the&nbsp;<a title="Luminosity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity">luminosity</a>&nbsp;of a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Celestial object" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_object">celestial object</a>, on a&nbsp;<a title="Logarithmic scale" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale">logarithmic</a>&nbsp;<a title="Magnitude (astronomy)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)">astronomical magnitude</a>&nbsp;scale. An object's absolute magnitude is defined to be equal to the&nbsp;<a title="Apparent magnitude" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude">apparent magnitude</a>&nbsp;that the object would have if it were viewed from a distance of exactly 10&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Parsecs" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsecs">parsecs</a>&nbsp;(32.6&nbsp;<a title="Light-year" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year">light-years</a>), with no&nbsp;<a title="Extinction (astronomy)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)">extinction</a>&nbsp;(or dimming) of its light due to absorption by&nbsp;<a title="Cosmic dust" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust">interstellar dust</a>&nbsp;particles.</dd>
<dt id="absolute_motion" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Absolute motion" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_motion">absolute motion</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary"></dd>
<dt id="absolute_pressure" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Absolute pressure" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_pressure">absolute pressure</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Is zero-referenced against a perfect vacuum, using an&nbsp;<a title="Absolute scale" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_scale">absolute scale</a>, so it is equal to gauge pressure plus atmospheric pressure.</dd>
<dt id="absolute_scale" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Absolute scale" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_scale">absolute scale</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Is a system of&nbsp;<a title="Measurement" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement">measurement</a>&nbsp;that begins at a minimum, or zero point, and progresses in only one direction. An absolute scale differs from an arbitrary, or "relative," scale, which begins at some point selected by a person and can progress in both directions. An absolute scale begins at a natural minimum, leaving only one direction in which to progress.</dd>
<dt id="absolute_zero" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Absolute zero" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero">absolute zero</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The theoretical lowest possible&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#temperature"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;temperature">temperature</span></a>, understood by international agreement as equivalent to 0&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#kelvin"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;Kelvin">Kelvin</span></a>&nbsp;or &minus;273.15&nbsp;&deg;C (&minus;459.67&nbsp;&deg;F). More formally, it is the theoretical lower limit of the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#thermodynamic_temperature"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;thermodynamic temperature">thermodynamic temperature</span></a>&nbsp;scale, at which&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#enthalpy"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;enthalpy">enthalpy</span></a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#entropy"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;entropy">entropy</span></a>&nbsp;of a cooled&nbsp;<a title="Ideal gas" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas">ideal gas</a>&nbsp;reach their minimum values and the fundamental particles of nature have minimal vibrational motion.</dd>
<dt id="absorption_spectroscopy" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Absorption spectroscopy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy">absorption spectroscopy</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Refers to&nbsp;<a title="Spectroscopy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a>&nbsp;techniques that measure the&nbsp;<a title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)">absorption</a>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<a title="Electromagnetic radiation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation">radiation</a>, as a function of&nbsp;<a title="Frequency" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency">frequency</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Wavelength" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength">wavelength</a>, due to its interaction with a sample. The sample absorbs energy, i.e., photons, from the radiating field. The intensity of the absorption varies as a function of frequency, and this variation is the&nbsp;<a title="Absorption spectroscopy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy#Absorption_spectrum">absorption spectrum</a>. Absorption spectroscopy is performed across the&nbsp;<a title="Electromagnetic spectrum" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>.</dd>
<dt id="absorptivity" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Absorbance" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorbance">absorptivity</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary"></dd>
<dt id="accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Accelerating expansion of the universe" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe">accelerating expansion of the universe</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Is the observation that the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Metric expansion of space" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space">expansion</a>&nbsp;of the&nbsp;<a title="Universe" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe">universe</a>&nbsp;is such that the velocity at which a distant galaxy is receding from the observer is continuously increasing with time.</dd>
<dt id="acceleration" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Acceleration" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration">acceleration</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The rate at which the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#velocity"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;velocity">velocity</span></a>&nbsp;of a body changes with time.</dd>
<dt id="acceleration_due_to_gravity" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Gravitational acceleration" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration">acceleration due to gravity</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The acceleration on an object caused by the force of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#gravitation"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;gravitation">gravitation</span></a>.</dd>
<dt id="accelerometer" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Accelerometer" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerometer">accelerometer</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">An instrument used to measure the proper&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#acceleration"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;acceleration">acceleration</span></a>&nbsp;of a body irrespective of other forces.</dd>
<dt id="acoustics" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Acoustics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustics">acoustics</a></dfn></dt>

Contrast&nbsp;<em><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#cohesion"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;cohesion">cohesion</span></a></em>.</dd>
<dt id="adiabatic_cooling" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Adiabatic cooling" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_cooling">adiabatic cooling</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary"></dd>
<dt id="adiabatic_heating" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Adiabatic heating" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_heating">adiabatic heating</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary"></dd>
<dt id="adiabatic_process" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Adiabatic process" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_process">adiabatic process</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Occurs without transfer of&nbsp;<a title="Heat" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat">heat</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Mass" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass">mass</a>&nbsp;of substances between a&nbsp;<a title="Thermodynamic system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system">thermodynamic system</a>&nbsp;and its surroundings. In an adiabatic process, energy is transferred to the surroundings only as&nbsp;<a title="Work (thermodynamics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)">work</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Carath&eacute;odory_5-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#cite_note-Carath%C3%A9odory-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bailyn_21_6-0" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#cite_note-Bailyn_21-6">[6]</a></sup>&nbsp;The adiabatic process provides a rigorous conceptual basis for the theory used to expound the&nbsp;<a title="First law of thermodynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a>, and as such it is a key concept in&nbsp;<a title="Thermodynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>.</dd>
<dt id="aerodynamics" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Aerodynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Is the study of the motion of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Air" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air">air</a>, particularly its interaction with a solid object, such as an&nbsp;<a title="Airplane" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airplane">airplane</a>&nbsp;wing. It is a sub-field of&nbsp;<a title="Fluid dynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Gas dynamics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_dynamics">gas dynamics</a>, and many aspects of aerodynamics theory are common to these fields.</dd>
<dt id="afocal_system" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Afocal system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afocal_system">afocal system</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">In&nbsp;<a title="Optics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics">optics</a>&nbsp;an&nbsp;<strong>afocal system</strong>&nbsp;(a system without focus) is an optical system that produces no net convergence or divergence of the beam, i.e. has an infinite&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Effective focal length" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_focal_length">effective focal length</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>&nbsp;This type of system can be created with a pair of optical elements where the distance between the elements is equal to the sum of each element's&nbsp;<a title="Focal length" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focal_length">focal length</a>&nbsp;(<em>d</em>&nbsp;=&nbsp;<em>f</em><sub>1</sub>+<em>f</em><sub>2</sub>).</dd>
<dt id="air_mass_(astronomy)" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Air mass (astronomy)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_mass_(astronomy)">air mass (astronomy)</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">In&nbsp;<a title="Astronomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy">astronomy</a>,&nbsp;<strong>air mass</strong>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<strong>airmass</strong>&nbsp;is the "amount of air that one is looking through"&nbsp;when seeing a star or other celestial source below&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's atmosphere" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>. It is formulated as the integral of air density along the light&nbsp;<a title="Ray (optics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(optics)">ray</a>.</dd>
<dt id="air_mass_(meteorology)" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Air mass" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_mass">air mass (meteorology)</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">In&nbsp;<a title="Meteorology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology">meteorology</a>, an&nbsp;<strong>air mass</strong>&nbsp;is a volume of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Air" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air">air</a>&nbsp;defined by its&nbsp;<a title="Temperature" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature">temperature</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Water vapor" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor">water vapor</a>&nbsp;content. Air masses cover many hundreds or thousands of miles and adapt to the characteristics of the surface below them. They are classified according to latitude and their continental or maritime source regions. Colder air masses are termed polar or arctic, while warmer air masses are deemed tropical. Continental and superior air masses are dry while maritime and monsoon air masses are moist.&nbsp;<a title="Weather front" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_front">Weather fronts</a>&nbsp;separate air masses with different density (temperature and/or moisture) characteristics. Once an air mass moves away from its source region, underlying vegetation and water bodies can quickly modify its character. Classification schemes tackle an air mass' characteristics, as well as modification.</dd>

<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Angular momentum" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum">angular momentum</a></dfn>(Rarely, moment of momentum or rotational momentum) is the rotational equivalent of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Linear momentum" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_momentum">linear momentum</a>. It is an important quantity in physics because it is a&nbsp;<a title="Conservation law" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law">conserved quantity</a>&mdash;the total angular momentum of a closed system remains constant.</p>
<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Angular velocity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity">angular velocity</a></dfn>Refers to how fast an object rotates or revolves relative to another point, i.e. how fast the angular position or orientation of an object changes with time. There are two types of angular velocity: orbital angular velocity and spin angular velocity. Spin angular velocity refers to how fast a rigid body rotates with respect to its centre of rotation. Orbital angular velocity refers to how fast a rigid body's centre of rotation revolves about a fixed origin, i.e. the time rate of change of its angular position relative to the origin. In general, angular velocity is measured in angle per unit time, e.g.&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Radians per second" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians_per_second">radians per second</a>. The&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="SI" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI">SI</a>&nbsp;unit of angular velocity is expressed as radians/sec with the radian having a dimensionless value of unity, thus the SI units of angular velocity are listed as&nbsp;<em>1/sec</em>. Angular velocity is usually represented by the symbol&nbsp;<a title="Omega" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omega">omega</a>&nbsp;(&omega;, sometimes &Omega;). By convention, positive angular velocity indicates counter-clockwise rotation, while negative is clockwise.</p>

Contrast&nbsp;<em><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#cation"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;cation">cation</span></a></em>.</p>
<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Annihilation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation">annihilation</a></dfn>In&nbsp;<a title="Particle physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics">particle physics</a>, annihilation is the process that occurs when a&nbsp;<a title="Subatomic particle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle">subatomic particle</a>&nbsp;collides with its respective&nbsp;<a title="Antiparticle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle">antiparticle</a>&nbsp;to produce other particles, such as an&nbsp;<a title="Electron" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron">electron</a>&nbsp;colliding with a&nbsp;<a title="Positron" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron">positron</a>&nbsp;to produce two&nbsp;<a title="Photon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon">photons</a>.&nbsp;The total&nbsp;<a title="Energy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy">energy</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Momentum" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum">momentum</a>&nbsp;of the initial pair are conserved in the process and distributed among a set of other particles in the final state. Antiparticles have exactly opposite additive&nbsp;<a title="Quantum number" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number">quantum numbers</a>&nbsp;from particles, so the sums of all quantum numbers of such an original pair are zero. Hence, any set of particles may be produced whose total quantum numbers are also zero as long as&nbsp;<a title="Conservation of energy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy">conservation of energy</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation of momentum" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum">conservation of momentum</a>&nbsp;are obeyed.</p>
<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Anode" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anode">anode</a></dfn>The electrode through which a conventional&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#current"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;current">electric current</span></a>&nbsp;flows into a polarized electrical device; the direction of current flow is, by convention, opposite to the direction of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#electron"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;electron">electron</span></a>&nbsp;flow, and so electrons flow&nbsp;<em>out of</em>&nbsp;the anode. In a&nbsp;<a title="Galvanic cell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galvanic_cell">galvanic cell</a>, the anode is the negative terminal or pole which emits electrons toward the external part of an&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#electrical_circuit"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;electrical circuit">electrical circuit</span></a>. However, in an&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#electrolytic_cell"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;electrolytic cell">electrolytic cell</span></a>, the anode is the wire or plate having excess&nbsp;<em>positive</em>&nbsp;charge, so named because negatively charged&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#anion"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;anion">anions</span></a>&nbsp;tend to move towards it. Contrast&nbsp;<em><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#cathode"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;cathode">cathode</span></a></em>.</p>
<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Anti-gravity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-gravity">anti-gravity</a></dfn>(Also known as&nbsp;<em>non-gravitational field</em>) is a theory of creating a place or object that is free from the force of&nbsp;<a title="Gravity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity">gravity</a>. It does not refer to the lack of weight under gravity experienced in&nbsp;<a title="Free fall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_fall">free fall</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Orbit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit">orbit</a>, or to balancing the force of gravity with some other force, such as electromagnetism or aerodynamic lift.</p>

<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Antineutron" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antineutron">antineutron</a>&nbsp;</dfn>Is the&nbsp;<a title="Antiparticle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle">antiparticle</a>&nbsp;of the&nbsp;<a title="Neutron" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron">neutron</a>. It differs from the neutron only in that some of its properties have&nbsp;<a title="Additive inverse" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse">equal magnitude but opposite sign</a>. It has the same&nbsp;<a title="Mass" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass">mass</a>&nbsp;as the neutron, and no net&nbsp;<a title="Electric charge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge">electric charge</a>, but has opposite&nbsp;<a title="Baryon number" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_number">baryon number</a>&nbsp;(+1 for neutron, &minus;1 for the antineutron). This is because the antineutron is composed of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Antiquark" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiquark">antiquarks</a>, while neutrons are composed of&nbsp;<a title="Quark" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark">quarks</a>. The antineutron consists of one&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Up antiquark" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_antiquark">up antiquark</a>&nbsp;and two&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Down antiquark" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_antiquark">down antiquarks</a>.</p>
<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Antiparticle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle">antiparticle</a></dfn>In&nbsp;<a title="Particle physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics">particle physics</a>, every type of&nbsp;<a title="Particle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle">particle</a>&nbsp;has an associated antiparticle with the same&nbsp;<a title="Mass" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass">mass</a>&nbsp;but with opposite&nbsp;<a title="Charge (physics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_(physics)">physical charges</a>&nbsp;(such as&nbsp;<a title="Electric charge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge">electric charge</a>). For example, the antiparticle of the&nbsp;<a title="Electron" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron">electron</a>&nbsp;is the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Antielectron" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antielectron">antielectron</a>&nbsp;(which is often referred to as&nbsp;<em>positron</em>). While the electron has a negative electric charge, the positron has a positive electric charge, and is produced naturally in certain types of&nbsp;<a title="Radioactive decay" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay">radioactive decay</a>. The opposite is also true: the antiparticle of the positron is the electron. Some particles, such as the&nbsp;<a title="Photon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon">photon</a>, are their own antiparticle. Otherwise, for each pair of antiparticle partners, one is designated as normal matter (the kind all matter usually interacted with is made of), and the other (usually given the prefix "anti-") as&nbsp;<em><a title="Antimatter" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter">antimatter</a></em>.</p>

<p><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Astronomical unit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit">astronomical unit</a></dfn>(Symbol:&nbsp;<strong>au</strong>,&nbsp;<strong>ua</strong>,&nbsp;or&nbsp;<strong>AU</strong>) is a&nbsp;<a title="Unit of length" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length">unit of length</a>, roughly the distance from&nbsp;<a title="Earth" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth">Earth</a>&nbsp;to the&nbsp;<a title="Sun" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun">Sun</a>. Originally conceived as the average of Earth's aphelion and perihelion, since 2012 it has been defined as exactly 149,597,870,700 metres, or about 150&nbsp;million kilometres (93&nbsp;million miles).&nbsp;The astronomical unit is used primarily for measuring distances within the&nbsp;<a title="Solar System" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System">Solar System</a>&nbsp;or around other stars. <dt id="black_body" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Black body" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body">black body</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A hypothetical idealized physical body that completely absorbs all incident&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#electromagnetic_radiation"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</span></a>, regardless of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#frequency"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;frequency">frequency</span></a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#angle_of_incidence"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;angle of incidence">angle of incidence</span></a>. Perfect black bodies are imagined as substitutes for actual physical bodies in many theoretical discussions of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#thermodynamics"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;thermodynamics">thermodynamics</span></a>, and the construction of nearly perfect black bodies in the real world remains a topic of interest for materials engineers. Contrast&nbsp;<em><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#white_body"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;white body">white body</span></a></em>.</dd>
<dt id="black-body_radiation" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Black-body radiation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body_radiation">black-body radiation</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The type of electromagnetic radiation within or surrounding a body in thermodynamic equilibrium with its environment, or emitted by a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#black_body"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;black body">black body</span></a>&nbsp;(an opaque and non-reflective body) held at constant, uniform temperature. The radiation has a specific spectrum and intensity that depends only on the temperature of the body.</dd>

<dt id="boiling_point" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Boiling point" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point">boiling point</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#temperature"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;temperature">temperature</span></a>&nbsp;at which a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#liquid"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;liquid">liquid</span></a>&nbsp;undergoes a phase change into a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#gas"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;gas">gas</span></a>; the vapour pressure of liquid and gas are equal at this temperature.</dd>
<dt id="boiling_point_elevation" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a class="mw-redirect" title="Boiling point elevation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point_elevation">boiling point elevation</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Describes the phenomenon that the&nbsp;<a title="Boiling point" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point">boiling point</a>&nbsp;of a&nbsp;<a title="Liquid" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid">liquid</a>&nbsp;(a&nbsp;<a title="Solvent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent">solvent</a>) will be higher when another compound is added, meaning that a&nbsp;<a title="Solution" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution">solution</a>&nbsp;has a higher boiling point than a pure solvent. This happens whenever a non-volatile solute, such as a salt, is added to a pure solvent, such as water. The boiling point can be measured accurately using an&nbsp;<a title="Ebullioscope" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebullioscope">ebullioscope</a>.</dd>
<dt id="boltzmann_constant" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Boltzmann constant" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant">Boltzmann constant</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A physical constant relating the average&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#kinetic_energy"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;kinetic energy">kinetic energy</span></a>&nbsp;of the particles in a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#gas"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;gas">gas</span></a>&nbsp;with the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#temperature"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;temperature">temperature</span></a>&nbsp;of the gas. It is the gas constant R divided by the Avogadro constant NA.</dd>

<dt id="boson" class="glossary"><dfn class="glossary"><a title="Boson" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson">boson</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A type of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#particle"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;particle">particle</span></a>&nbsp;that behaves according to&nbsp;<a title="Bose&ndash;Einstein statistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics">Bose&ndash;Einstein statistics</a>&nbsp;and possesses integer&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#spin"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;spin">spin</span></a>. &sect;gluon">gluons</span></a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#w_and_Z_bosons"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;W and Z bosons">W and Z bosons</span></a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#higgs_boson"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry on this page at &sect;Higgs boson">Higgs bosons</span></a>, and the hypothetical&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_physics#graviton"><span id="" class="glossary-link-internal" title="See entry
